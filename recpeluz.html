pinakbet

A variety of local vegetables, including bitter melon, yard-long beans, okra, squash, and eggplant, make up pinakbet. The mixture of tomatoes, pork, and especially the shrimp/fish paste give it a distinct flavor.
The most requested delicious and nutritrious dish in the Philippines "Pinakbet" Pinakbet or pakbet has its roots in the Northern Luzon Region, where the name is a contraction of the Ilokano word "pinakebbet," which means withered or shriveled. Ilokano is one of the few dialects in the Philippines. As generation pass, many versions have made one of this is the Pakbet Tagalog. Pakbet Tagalog and the OG Pinakbet are just similar the only different is the method of cooking.
Benefits of the dish. has many health advantages. It is incorporated with a range of brilliant, colorful veggies that are high in healthy vitamins and minerals for your body. Everyone ought to try this dish because it has so many health advantages.

pinakbet Ilocano recipe

Ingredients

  1. 4 pcs round eggplant
  2. 2 pcs small bitter melon
  3. 1/2 bundle string beans
  4. 1 pc sweet potato
  5. 8 pcs okra
  6. 1 pc tomato
  7. 1 pc onion
  8. 1 1/2 cup water
  9. 1 lb pork belly
  10. 1/4 cup Bagoong isda

Instruction

  1. Heat cooking oil in a pot. Saute onion and garlic. Add ginger and continue to cook until the onion softens.
  2. pan fry the pork belly until golden brown
  3. Pour water. Let boil.
  4. Add tomato and bagoong alamang. Stir. Cover the pot. Cook for 3 minutes.
  5. Put the kalabasa and kamote into the pot. Cook for 7 minutes.
  6. Add sitaw, okra, ampalaya, and eggplant. Stir. Cover the pot and cook for 5 minutes.
  7. Season with ground black pepper and add remaining lechon kawali. Cook for minutes.
  8. Tranfer to a serving plate. Serve. Share and Enjoy!

igado

Igado is a classic Ilocano meal made with pork tenderloin and pig guts like liver, kidney, and heart. In my opinion, this is one of the best Filipino dishes available. Some people, though, may need to develop a taste for it. There are numerous ways to define Filipino cuisine.

This is a one-of-a-kind spiced dish created with a range of seasonings and a few vegetables to complement the meat ingredients. It is also well-known for its utilization of pig innards, as previously stated. However, we shall delve deeper into the ingredients for this recipe later on. According to studies, Igado was influenced by Spanish cuisine, as you may have guessed from the name. "Higado" means "liver" in Spanish, and some claim that Filipinos misheard it as "Igado," thus the current name.

igado ilocano recipe

Ingredients

  1. 1/2 lbs. pork tenderloin
  2. 1/2 lb. pork liver
  3. 1/2 lb. pork kidney
  4. 1/4 lb pig liver
  5. 1 1/2 tsp salt
  6. 1/2 tsp gorund black pepper
  7. 1 red bell pepper
  8. 1/2 cup green peas
  9. 1 carrot
  10. 3 laurel leaves
  11. 1 onion
  12. 5 cloves garlic minced
  13. 6 tbsp soy sauce
  14. 5 tbsp white vinegar
  15. 1 1/4 cup water
  16. 4 tbsp cooking oil

Instruction

  1. Boil the kidney with 4 thumbs of ginger for at least 30 minutes. Let it cool down and slice into 2-inch strips. Set aside.
  2. Heat 2 tablespoons of cooking oil in a pan. Sauté the pork liver for 2 minutes. Remove from the pan and set aside.
  3. Pour the remaining oil in the pan. Once hot, sauté garlic and onion until the latter softens.
  4. Add the pork kidney. Cook for 1 minute.
  5. Add the pork tenderloin. Sauté until the outer layer turns light brown.
  6. Pour the soy sauce and vinegar into the pan. Let the liquid boil. Stir.
  7. Add the laurel leaves and pour water. Let boil. Reduce the heat between low to medium setting. Continue cooking for 40 minutes.
  8. Add the liver back into the pan. Stir and continue cooking for 3 minutes.
  9. Put the bell pepper, carrot, and green peas.
  10. Season with salt and ground black pepper. Cook for 2 minutes. Transfer to a serving bowl. Serve hot with rice.
  11. Share and enjoy!

tupig

Tupig, also known as intemtem or kangkanen, is a Filipino rice cake native to northwestern Luzon, specifically Pangasinan, Tarlac, and Ilocos. It is made from ground slightly fermented soaked glutinous rice mixed with coconut milk, muscovado sugar, and young coconut strips. Intemtem or Tupig is a popular native delicacy from Pangasinan that is wrapped in banana leaves and cooked over charcoal. Traditionally, this rice cake was exclusively provided at Christmas and New Year's. It was also claimed that it was widely given to carollers at Christmas (similar to sweet sweets given on Halloween in the West).

tupig recipe

Ingredients

  1. 2 cups glutinous rice
  2. 1 cup young coconut meat
  3. 1 cup coconut milk
  4. 1/2 cup brown sugar
  5. banana leaves (for packing)

Instruction

  1. In a small mixing bowl add glutinous rice and pour enough water to soak the rice. Leave it in a room temperature overnight.
  2. Drain the water from the rice then place in a cheesecloth to remove excess liquid.
  3. Place in a blender or food processor and process until it becomes a powder, add the coconut milk and sugar and continue to blend until even in consistency.
  4. Place mixture in a bowl then combine with the coconut strips.
  5. Place 2 tablespoons of mixture in the centre of the banana leaf then wrap by rolling and fold the edges to avoid dripping. (This will be tricky as the dough is very runny, it takes practice).
  6. Immediately place in a charcoal or any grill once you wrap one, cook by turning frequently. Once the banana leaf is charred on all sides remove it from the grill then set aside to cool before serving

pinapaitan

Beef Papaitan, also known as Papaitang Baka, is a variation on the popular Pinapaitan that uses the internal organs of a cow. Papaitan baka, a traditional meal from the Ilocos area, is noted for its distinct flavor profile and rich cultural legacy.

This meal, traditionally made using goat innards, gets its name from the addition of goat bile, which adds a nice bitterness that enriches the entire flavor. In this recipe, we'll look at how to make a delicious variation of papaitan baka with cow innards as a substitute. Join us as we walk you through the steps of making this delectable Ilokano delicacy.

beef pinapaitan recipe

Ingredients

  1. 1/2 kilo beef internal organs tripe, liver, kidney, heart, pancreas, intestines
  2. 1/4 kilo beef tenderloin cut into 1/2" cubes
  3. 1/4 cup beef bile or apdo
  4. 1 pc medium size onion chopped
  5. 1 head garlic minced
  6. 1 pc ginger about 1 inch, slivered
  7. 1/4 cup chopped spring onion
  8. 2 pc red chili pepper or siling labuyo
  9. 3 Tbsp. salt
  10. 1/2 tsp. salt
  11. 1/2 tsp. ground pepper
  12. 1 Tbsp. patis or fish sauce

Instruction

  1. Wash and mash the internal organs with banana leaves with 3 Tbsp. salt to remove the musky odor. Make sure that the intestines are very clean.
  2. Boil the internal organs on a big pot until tender then remove from liquid and cut into 1/2 inch cubes. Set aside.
  3. Saute ginger, garlic and onion. Stir fry until the onions are cooked and add the internal organs.
  4. Stir fry again until some broth comes out of the internal organs.
  5. Season with salt and pepper and adjust the amount according to your desired taste.
  6. Stir fry for a few minutes then add some water, just enough to cover all the ingredients.
  7. Simmer and add the beef tenderloin until the tenderloin is cooked.
  8. Drop the red chili peppers and pour the bile little by little to prevent the dish from becoming too bitter.
  9. Season with patis and adjust the amount according to your preference.
  10. Serve hot and garnish with chopped spring onions.

dinakdakan

Dinakdakan is an Ilocano delicacy comprised of barbecued pig pieces tossed with onions and chili peppers in a calamansi sauce. It's great as an appetizer with your favorite drinks or as a main course with steamed rice.

Pig's brain is used in the genuine dinakdakan recipe. This ingredient contributes to the dish's thick and sticky texture. We're not going to be as brave in this recipe feature because I couldn't get pig brain in the Northern Suburbs of Chicago, so we'll use mayonnaise. This dinakdakan dish is delicious.

It is essential to ensure that each component is soft after boiling. You don't want your food to be rough and difficult to eat. It is critical to grill the meat. Your meal will be more tender. This will make eating more enjoyable.

dinakdakan recipe

Ingredients

  1. 1 lb pork face
  2. 1 lb pork ears
  3. 2 pcs bay leaves
  4. 8 oz pig brain
  5. 6 oz pork liver
  6. 3 tsp salt
  7. 1 tsp pepper corns
  8. 1/4 cup calamnsi juice
  9. water
  10. 2 green and red chili
  11. 1 red onion
  12. 4 tbsp white vinegar
  13. 1 thumb-size ginger
  14. mayonnaise

Instruction

  1. Pour 6 to 8 cups water in a cooking pot. Let boil.
  2. Once the water starts to boil, you have the option to add dried bay leaves and whole peppercorn. Add-in the pig ears and face. Set the heat to low and continue to boil for 50 to 60 minutes.
  3. Discard the water and let the excess water drip. Rub a little bit of salt all over the boiled ears and face. Rub the ginger powder on the liver.
  4. Heat-up the grill. Grill the ears and face for 4 to 6 minutes per side or until it turns a bit crisp, but not burnt. Grill the liver for 5 to 8 minutes depending on the thickness.
  5. Remove the grilled pig parts from the grill. Let it cool down and start chopping into bite-size pieces.
  6. Meanwhile, combine mayonnaise and vinegar in mixing bowl. Stir.
  7. Add some ground black pepper. Continue to stir until the ingredients are well blended.
  8. Add the ginger, chili, onion, and garlic powder (optional). Toss.
  9. Add more salt if needed. Transfer to a serving bowl. Serve.
  10. Share and enjoy!

dinengdeng

Dinengdeng (also known as inabraw) is a Filipino delicacy of the Ilocano people, comparable to pinakbet. It is classified as a dish based on bagoong monamon soup. Dinengdeng, unlike pinakbet, features fewer veggies and more bagoong monamon soup base.

It could be the main dish or viand that goes with the innapuy (steamed rice), or it could be a companion dish with meat dishes like adobo, lechon, caldereta, or the inevitable pinapaitan, sinanglaw, or sangkutsar, the presence of dinengdeng is a must as it somehow "balances" the food between what's said to be "healthy" and what's said to be "unhealthy," However, the dinengdeng is frequently desired, the "most attacked," particularly by people who are conscious of their "heart" or "liver" or of their waistlines.

Native dinengdeng recipe

Ingredients

  1. 1 whole Bangus Fish
  2. 2 cups Malunggay
  3. 1 bundle saluyot leaves (White jute)
  4. 3 pc Squash flower
  5. 5-9 pcs okra
  6. 1 bundle string beans
  7. 2 pcs ampalaya(bitter gourd)
  8. 2 1/2 tbsp bagoong isda (unprocessed fish sauce)
  9. 1 knob ginger
  10. 1 onion
  11. 2 pcs tomato
  12. 3 cups water

Instruction

  1. Bring water to a boil in a large cooking pot.
  2. Add the ginger, onion, and tomato. Cook covered for 5 minutes.
  3. Pour-in the bagoong isda. Stir.
  4. Add okra and string beans. Stir and add the ampalaya. Cook in medium heat for 7 to 10 minutes.
  5. Put-in the squash flower and malunggay leaves. Cook for 3 to 5 minutes.
  6. Add the grilled milk fish. Let it stay for 3 to 5 minutes to add flavor to the dish.
  7. Serve. Share and enjoy!

Laing

Laing (or Natong) is a spicy meal made with dried taro leaves and coconut milk (or cream). This developed in the Philippines' Bicol area (the southernmost section of the island of Luzon). This meal got popular as a result of its delicious taste and low cost.

It could be the main dish or viand that goes with the innapuy (steamed rice), or it could be a companion dish with meat dishes like adobo, lechon, caldereta, or the inevitable pinapaitan, sinanglaw, or sangkutsar, the presence of dinengdeng is a must as it somehow "balances" the food between what's said to be "healthy" and what's said to be "unhealthy," However, the dinengdeng is frequently desired, the "most attacked," particularly by people who are conscious of their "heart" or "liver" or of their waistlines.

laing recipe

Ingredients

  1. 5-7 pcs taro leaves
  2. 1/2 lb pork meat
  3. 1/2 lb shrimp
  4. 3 pc Squash flower
  5. 5-9 pcs okra
  6. 1/2 cup shrimp paste
  7. 1 onion
  8. 6 chili pepper/ labuyo
  9. 1 knob ginger
  10. 1 onion
  11. 2 cloves garlic
  12. 4 cups coconut milk
  13. 4tbsp fish sauce
  14. 2 tsp gorund black pepper

Instruction

  1. In a large pan, pour-in the coconut milk and bring to a boil
  2. Add the garlic, onion, and ginger. Cook for 10 minutes
  3. Add the pork meat and shrimp paste and stir continuously
  4. Put in the chili and simmer for another 10 minutes
  5. Add the fish sauce and dried Taro leaves and simmer for 40 to 60. Add water if needed.
  6. Put the shrimps in and cook for for 5 minutes. Season with ground black pepper.
  7. Serve. Share and enjoy!
